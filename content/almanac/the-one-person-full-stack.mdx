Most software does not fail in the writing. It fails in the seams: the gap
between what product meant and what the back-end shipped, between the design and
the thing that reached production. Every handoff is a place where intent leaks.

A team of three specialists can be wonderful. It can also spend more effort
staying in sync than building. When the work is a first version, an MVP, or a
product still deciding what it wants to be, one accountable partner who shapes,
builds, and ships tends to move faster and leave fewer seams behind.

## Where one head wins

- **No translation tax.** The person scoping the feature is the person building
  the API and the interface. Nothing gets lost on the way.
- **Decisions stay coherent.** The stack, the data model, and the UI grow from
  one point of view, so the whole thing reads as one thing.
- **Faster loops.** Working software lands in your hands early and often,
  because there is no queue between disciplines.

## Where it does not

This is not an argument against teams. Past a certain scale, or when the work
splits cleanly into deep specialisms, more hands help. The trick is knowing
which season you are in. Early, a single gardener keeps the whole plant healthy.
Later, you bring people in to tend the parts that have grown large.
